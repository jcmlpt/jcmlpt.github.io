Introduction

I'll talk about the motivations for this work and how the presentation is structured.
Current route generators already provide help for pedestrians regarding time estimating. However, routes often matter more than just the time to be travelled and each person has different personal motivations and physical conditions.
As a contribute in this direction, the model we bring doesn’t look for the route with most air conditioning like the chicken in the cartoon, but will consider other elements.

Walking conditions

Personal motivations

Route generation

New outputs

We will consider slope, sun exposition, and integration as walking conditions to route generation, all to address different and possible personal motivations and physical limitations. We used integration as an possibility, not to take a side in closeness vs betweenness.
Our model will generate original routes considering these walking conditions and meeting the personal motivations.
Other than the usual outputs, time and distance, the model will also calculate the average integration and total energy expenditure for each generated route. We believe these tools can further increase their role promoting walking.

Pedestrian
crossings

 

The pedestrian crossings are divided into: signalized, zebra and informal crossings.
For zebra and informal crossings we considered that there was no waiting time but only a three second delay caused by interrupting and resuming walking.
For most signalized crossings the local authorities use dynamic times and do not store the green and waiting times.
One source used 11 seconds as waiting time for signalized crossings, but several of these crossings are divided in different parts. Since we don’t know the green times we are unable to identify the situations where there may exist stops along a crossing, using such waiting time value could penalize the model too much. Without a correct way to characterize the signalized crosswalks, these were treated as the remaining crossings – three second delay in each part of the crossing.

Digital terrain model

A digital terrain model is needed to add elevation to the network.

A digital terrain model is needed to add elevation to the network, which is necessary to calculate the slope and walking speed.
Represented as a raster, the size of its cells is important. If it has insufficient spatial resolution it could result in too many smaller segments with no slope. And segments too close to the cell limits could end up with an incorrect slope. At the end, a new DTM was created with one meter resolution, 100 times bigger resolution than the initially available one.

Walking speed

The walking speed was calculated according to the slope, using Tobler’s equation (1993). Additionally, the speed for walking up and down stairs was calculated following Fruin’s work (1987).

In the next step, to calculate the walking speed we used the Tobler equation that considers any slope. For a null slope the result of the equation is close to the values found in several sources.
Fruin mentions that the average speed of climbing stairs is about one third of the walking speed without any slope, and that going down the stairs speed increases by 10%.

Testing the model

Three different sets of tests were carried out as a proof of concept of how the model should be validated in the future.

Walking time

The generated routes' walking time was compared with Google Maps’ (2017) walking time.

For several routes the walking time was compared with Google Maps’ walking time. From all routes generated in the case studies, 20 unique ones were identified and created in Google Maps, using when necessary additional destinations to force the route’s geometry.
Five routes were unable to be reproduced due to the absence of Av. Praia da Vitória at the time. With a sample of 15 routes the average difference between the model and Google Maps is 35s or 3.89% with a standard deviation of 24s or 2.23%.

Energy expenditure

The model’s energy expenditure results were compared with the results of two different commercial solutions, Samsung Gear Fit smartwatch and MapMyWalk (2017) fitness tracking application.

The model’s energy expenditure results were compared with the results of two different commercial solutions, Samsung Gear Fit smartwatch and MapMyWalk fitness tracking application.
Eight different routes (four in both directions) were measured several times with either solution. 15 measurements were used and compared giving an average difference of 8kilocalorie or 13.29% and a standard deviation of 8kcal or 12.54%.

Shading

To compare the shading measures no other model or application was found, and therefore the model results were validated on site.

To compare the shading measures no other model or application was found, and the model results were validated on site with several visits to distinct locations at different times.
The results were mostly positive with 54 locations having shaded pedestrian paths similar to the model results, and in only two locations the same cannot be said.

Conclusions

Achievements:

.Walking conditions

.Personal motivations

.New routes and outputs

.Testing

We used integration, slopes and sun exposition as walking conditions.
These addressed possible personal motivations and were used to define the routing process.
We created original routes with these walking conditions. And new outputs, such as integration and energy expenditure.
And our testing showed that not only we can create the same routes as Google Maps, but also that these and the new ones we created have similar times as Google Maps'.

Future work:

.Integration

.Shading

.Crosswalks

.Energy expenditure

.Model validation

We only explored starting ideas and surely integration values can be used in additional and different ways in the route creation algorithm, identifying and testing them should be considered as future work, as should identifying the users motivations behind these metrics.
The presence of shaded footpaths and its use brought relevant results and encourages the continuation of its development, since we only considered the shade created by the buildings.
The signalized crosswalks should be further developed since they were treated like the remaining crosswalks and may be missing relevant and useful characterization.
Energy expenditure is a crucial metric to inform the pedestrian. It will be important in works like ours to keep up with the necessary improvements on calculating this metric.
And more work needs to be done to validate the model.
